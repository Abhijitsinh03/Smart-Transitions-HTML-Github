Planning My Portfolio

Finding Your Heart: The Things That Describe You

The heart of your portfolio will be a series of statements about who you are and what your abilities are. You are a unique and valuable combination of talents, skills, values and life experiences. You’ve done plenty of warm-up exercises before getting to this point. Here are some links back to some of the work that will help you identify your core “Who I Am” statements.

In upcoming tasks, we will help you learn how to create and/or upload the following items which will form the beginning of your portfolio:

  • My Gifts Inventory
  • My Skills Inventory
  • My Passions Inventory
  • My Values Inventory
  • Drafting a Life Work Vision
  • My Personal Brand
  • My Work Brand Worksheet

It’s time to review these documents and the things you identified about yourself. Are there any areas that seem to dominate? For instance, do you like to work with people? Do you like to build things? Do you like to solve problems? Do you like animals? Do you like to be outdoors? Do you like to express yourself creatively?

Using a mix of your inventory worksheets, your Life-Work Vision statement, and your personal and work brand worksheets, make a list of statements that describe who you are. Narrow this list down to six to ten statements that strongly describe you and for which you can provide evidence.

Use the “Who I Am” worksheet below to create what will become a single page section header for your portfolio. Be direct and use complete sentences. For instance, “I am good at caring for sick people,” or “I have leadership ability.” Then, in the box below that, provide written evidence for your statement. This might include a brief explanation and a list of the experiences you have had in “health care” or “leadership.”

Then use the evidence planner box below that to list the portfolio documents you have or want to get in support of this claim about yourself. What you write in this box will remain on this planner page but not show up when you publish the “Who I Am” header page for your portfolio.

Next, try to find at least one photograph to support your statement. Continuing with the example above, you might include a photograph of yourself volunteering at a retirement facility or nursing home or leading a group of younger children on a camping trip.

There is a sample Who I Am worksheet from the actual one below it. that should make this process easier to understand. Take some time, and do these with care. They will be the core around which you organize your portfolio. All of the other evidence you gather will be used to support these descriptions of who you are.

After you complete your first “Who I Am” worksheet, when you click on the publish button it will generate a PDF version of this page that you can preview. It will live in your STO Gallery, ready to use to organize your digital portfolio or print out for your print portfolio.

You will want at least 4 “Who I Am” header pages to organize your portfolio contents. More than 10 is probably too much. You can create additional “Who I Am” header pages by clicking on the “New Who I Am Header Page” button and repeating the process. You can do this up to 10 times before a dialog box will ask you to delete a previous “Who I Am Page.” Don't worry about the order you do them in now. Just create them. You can always reorganize them later.

You can edit your “Who I Am” header pages by returning to this task and simply editing the text and the hitting the publish button again. This will replace your original with the edited version. Revising or updating your “Who I Am” header pages is a good idea, You may want to update with new photos and new sample experiences. Getting to know yourself is an ongoing process.
