Keeping a job Conflict Management

Keeping a job Conflict Management

  1. Dial down the volume: If someone is yelling at you, don’t do or say anything until they stop yelling. Instead of telling them to stop yelling, which will just provoke more of a fight, try calmly asking them to repeat what they just said more slowly.
  2. Don’t explain your point of view: I know this is a hard one because you’re pretty sure you’re right, but someone who is in fight mode will just hear your explanation as more of a challenge. They will think you are fighting with them or running away and trying to get out of being responsible. If you both were being managed by “cool,” rational brains, this might work, but at least one of you probably isn’t.
  3. Restate what you’ve heard: A great way of defusing someone who is in fight mode is to calmly restate what you heard them say. Remember that this doesn’t mean you agree with them, and you’re not admitting anything. You’re just letting them know that you heard them and are giving them a chance to clarify.
  4. Ask for guidance or clarity: A real powerful question in these kinds of situations is “What would you like me to do?” Or “What would you like me to do?” Notice the emphasis is on the words “like” or “do.” If you emphasize the words “what” or “you” or “me,” you’ll end up sounding snarky and prolong the fight.
  5. State what you want: Beginning with the words “I” or “I’d,” simply state what you’d like to see happen. “I like working here and I want to do a good job, so I’d like for us to come to a workable agreement.” Avoid telling someone what he or she is doing. That will just fan the flames of the conflict.
  6. Get acknowledgement of what you’ve agreed to: If it feels like you’ve reached a place of agreement, repeat what you’ve agreed to as a question. “So we agree that...”
  7. Avoid the need to have the last word: The need to have the last word is an aggressive move. Avoid it in yourself and ignore it in others. Remember that you should be thinking strategically about what you need or want in the long term.

And, by the way, these techniques are not just good for work. They can really transform your personal life as well.

Identify the following statements as true or false based on an employee who wants to manage conflict successfully:

The faster I come back with a reply in an argument, the better it will be.

If my heart is pounding and I’m taking shallow breaths, I’m probably about to make a bad decision. I need to take some slow deep breaths.

If someone yells at me, I yell back.

If I get the last word in, it means that I’ve won.

Restating what I’ve heard is a good way of calming a situation down.

Finding something upon which we can agree is a good way out of a tangled argument.
