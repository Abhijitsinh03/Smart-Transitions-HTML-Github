Letters of Recommendation

In an upcoming task you will get a form letter that you can customize and send to people who might provide you with a letter of recommendation. Here’s what to ask for and how to ask.

Use the request form you will get in a future task to ask for a one-page letter (more than one page is usually too much to read) addressed to you or addressed “To whom it may concern.” You can explain that you are building a personal presentation portfolio to help prepare you for college or jo searching.

Ask for the letter to be on letterhead stationery if that is possible (some teachers will have school stationery, others may not). Ask the person from whom you are seeking the letter to comment briefly on your performance and abilities. If appropriate, ask the writer to refer to specific projects or events on which you worked. If there are specific qualities you are trying to highlight as your work brand, you can ask a person to comment on those specific qualities.

Ask politely and give the person at least a week to write the letter. Be sensitive to how busy people are. Later in the school year can be a better time to ask for letters from teachers and coaches, but don’t wait until the very end of the year. It’s usually good to specify a pick-up date. Example: “Is it okay if I drop by to pick the letter up a week from Thursday?”

Who to Ask:

The people you might ask for a letter of recommendation usually fall into one of four broad categories.

  • Teachers

    • These should be teachers who have had you in class for more than just a few weeks. You are usually looking for a letter that attests to your academic and/or leadership abilities here.

  • Coaches, Mentors, or Activities Sponsors

    • If you have participated in a sport or club, your coach or sponsor might write a letter attesting to your participation and your commitment. If you’ve worked with a mentor, he or she might be willing to attest to your character.

  • Employers

    • If you have been a good employee and left under good circumstances, you can ask for a letter of recommendation confirming your work skills and reliability.

  • Community Leaders

    • If you are active in, youth groups, church groups, or other community activities, or if you do volunteer work, consider getting letters that establish your involvement and responsibility.

Don’t ask parents or family members or peers for letters of recommendation. They will generally not be taken seriously by people reviewing your portfolio. Also, sometimes an employer might reach out to a person writing a letter of recommendation in search of more details, so don't ever fake a letter of recommendation.
