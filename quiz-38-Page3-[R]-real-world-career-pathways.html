Real World Career Pathways

Military (Army, Navy, Air Force, Marine Corps, Coast Guard)

For some people, the military is a great way to learn a marketable skill, travel and see the world, complete an education, and learn self-discipline, but there are a couple of things you need to understand.

  1. Military officers (lieutenants, captains, commanders, majors, colonels, admirals, and generals, etc.) have at least 4 years of college, usually in an engineering field or a field the military identifies that it needs. Officers may perform tasks like flying jets and helicopters, but an officer’s primary role is leadership and managing people in combat and in preparation for combat. If you love fixing tanks, becoming an officer won’t give you the opportunity to do what you love.
  2. Junior enlisted personnel (seaman, airman, private) have little or no command authority and usually perform tasks for which they have received specialized training or have specialized jobs.
  3. Senior enlisted personnel (corporal, sergeant, petty officer, chief petty officer, master sergeant, etc.)may also have specialized jobs, but also manage junior enlisted personal.

If you are primarily interested in combat service, join the Army or the Marine Corps. Special Forces jobs may seem very exciting and even glamorous if you’ve grown up on action films and video games, but it is very tough to qualify for these highly competitive programs. Each of the services has it’s own variation of special forces, and far more applicants fail out of the selection process than succeed at it. It takes a special set of skills and attributes to become a part of a special forces team.

If you are serious about a military career, see a recruiter now! The military is getting more selective and is no longer just taking anyone. If all you have is a GED, they may require some additional college. A juvenile criminal record can prevent you from being accepted into the military and may require applying for an exemption. The first step in considering a military career is to take the Armed Service Vocational Aptitude Battery (ASVAB). Base on the results of that test your recruiter will suggest different training options for which you qualify. If the military is part of your plan, find out everything you can about getting in now.
