My Skills

What skills do you possess? Remember that skills (as opposed to talents) are things you have learned to do. A talent and a skill might be connected. A natural gift for fixing mechanical things might become the skill of repairing cars, for instance. But a skill is something you’ve practiced and dedicated yourself to improving. Being clear and honest about your skills and your skill level can help you identify the best possible career path and can be something of value to offer a potential employer.

There are six “Skills” blocks below. You may have more skills than that, but for now, let’s focus on your six strongest or most developed skills. If you don't have six skills at this point, complete as many as you can. But you will need to identify at least two skills before moving on. There are three steps to completing each skill block and this task:

  1. Write out each skill in a complete sentence. For example: “I play basketball at the varsity level.” Be specific! For example don’t say “I play a musical instrument.” Instead say “I play acoustic and electric guitar.”
  2. What could you offer as proof of the skills you are claiming? Preferably this would be something that could fit into your personal presentation portfolio. Awards and certificates are always nice. If you’ve gone through some kind of special training program, there is usually some kind of document that attests to your participation and completion. If it is appropriate, you could present actual work to demonstrate your skill. Photographs can show projects on which you have worked. Letters from employers, customers, teachers or coaches can be offered as evidence.
  3. Rate your skill level in comparison to other students your age or grade level.
