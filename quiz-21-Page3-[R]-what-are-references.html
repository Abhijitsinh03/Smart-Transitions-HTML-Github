What Are References?

Things to avoid.

  1. Never use a reference without their permission. Just don’t do it.
  2. Don't use family members as references, especially if they share your last name. Employers tend to discount those kinds of references.
  3. Don't use your friends or peers as references. They will not be reliable or consistent.
  4. Remember that your references will be asked how they know you. Usually this is not a problem, but do you really want to list your parole officer as a reference?
  5. Finally, some people just won't answer a number from someone they don’t recognize and some people are hostile and suspicious about being called for things like this. Make sure your references know how important this is to you and that they are willing to make an effort. The last thing you want is to have an employer be unable to reach any of your references or have a bad experience with one of them.

To complete this reading, answer the question below (and yes, you can scroll back and look for the right answer if you need to).

Identify the following statements as true or false based on the reading above:

  1. References are people in the community who would say something positive about you and attest to your character as a person and/or an employee if called by a potential employer.

  2. Once you have a list of references, they should last you for years.

  3. Someone who has employed you or has some experience of you as a worker or an employee or a volunteer would be a good reference.

  4. Peers and family members make good references.

  5. If someone doesn't want to be a reference, try really hard to convince them.

  6. The best reference is someone who is enthusiastic about talking about your best qualities.
