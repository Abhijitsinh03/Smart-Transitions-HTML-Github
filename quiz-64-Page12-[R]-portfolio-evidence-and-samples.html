Portfolio Evidence and Samples

Odds and Ends

The question often comes up of whether to put your grades or test scores into your portfolio. The answer is, it depends. If you are still in school or just out of school good grades might say something important about you. At some point, however, actual grades are less and less important. Remember that the whole point of a portfolio is to create a better, more complete picture of who you are. When deciding on whether to include grades in your portfolio, you might consider the following based on what kind of grades you have:

  • Basically Good Grades (A’s, B’s, and maybe some C’s)

    • You’re may want to include these in your portfolio. You should be proud of them.

  • Mixed Bag (some A’s and B’s mixed in with some D’s and F’s)

    • You have a couple of choices. You could include them without emphasizing them, putting more emphasis on other contents. You could leave them out completely. Or you could list only the subjects in which you did well and include those grades. If you choose this option, be prepared to give an honest answer about your other grades if the question comes up.

  • Average Across the Board (pretty much straight C’s)

    • If you include grades like this, your portfolio should show a range of non-academic areas in which you excel.

  • Bad Grades (you know what they are)

    • It would be best to leave these out of your portfolio altogether, but if you are smart enough to be working on a portfolio, you are smart enough to pull up those grades!

Standardized Test Scores

The strategy above generally applies to standardized test scores as well, although they are a little safer to include because most people won’t understand them anyway. (Note: College admissions counselors will know what SATs and ACTs mean.) If your portfolio is purely focused on employment, you can leave out test scores.

High School Diploma of GED or College Diploma

If you have one, make a copy of it and put it in your portfolio.

Remember that a portfolio is designed to make you look good and to show off and emphasize your best qualities. This isn’t a scrapbook. All the items listed above, all the items that you put in your portfolio must be in there for a clear reason. They must highlight or document a talent, skill, passion, interest, value, or relevant life-work experience.

Identify the following statements as true or false based on the reading above:

  1. Make sure to give a person adequate time to write your letter of recommendation and schedule a time to pick it up.

  2. If you can't think of who to ask for a letter of recommendation, ask your parent or a friend to do it.

  3. Certificates show you have completed a training program in some specific skill, usually something of your choosing.

  4. You should have lots of random selfies in your portfolio.

  5. A writing sample can demonstrate that you have the ability to communicate well in writing.

  6. Nobody cares about your grades, so don't ever put them in your portfolio.
